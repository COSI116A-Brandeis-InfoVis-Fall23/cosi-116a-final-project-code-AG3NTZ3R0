Graduate Group 4: Analysis of Relationship Between Poverty and Racial Demographics with Housing Inequality in the U.S.A., COSI 116A F23

Brandon Lacy, Wantong Lin, Christine Nguyen, Kaiyu Peng

Project-long Course Project as part of COSI 116A: Information Visualization, taught by Prof. Dylan Cashman, Brandeis University.

Motivation

We have set out to create an interactive and educational visualization tool that addresses the organizational need for a straightforward way to understand the complex relationship of poverty and racial demographics with housing inequality in the United States. The tool allows users to explore and compare different household conditions through an interactive floor plan with color-coded items which represent income and expenses, thereby transforming abstract data into a vivid, tangible experience that powerfully conveys the realities of housing inequality.

Data Analysis

Our project uses data from the American Community Survey Supplemental Poverty Measure (ACS SPM) from 2009 to 2019, focusing on demographics, economics, and housing to understand poverty-related trends. The dataset, comprising 47 columns, includes categorical, ordinal, and quantitative types. We aggregated this data into averages for each racial demographic, which was crucial for dynamically generating furniture in the floor plan of our tool. Specific features from the dataset were selected to represent different furniture pieces, illustrating various household conditions. Accompanying the data, a dictionary provided clear explanations of each column, enhancing our understanding. Extensive data processing was necessary, revealing patterns and trends not immediately visible in the raw data, and aiding in the creation of our visualizations.

Task Analysis

The main task of our visualization is to discover the correlation between housing inequality and poverty trends among diverse racial backgrounds in the United States. We also want to present the impact and variations introduced by other demographic factors on this relationship, particularly on people's housing choices and economic situation. Users are able to derive personalized insights through exploring the nuanced economic dynamics within a unit across different demographic and housing scenarios.

Design Process

Our multi-family home view provides an engaging, clear visual representation of the data, with a customizable focus. The floor plan view provides a different perspective on the data and allows for clear comparisons between trends within different racial demographics. The radar chart view displays the six critical dimensions of housing inequality in a dynamic and informative way, revealing intersections and variations in inequality within and between the dimensions. Our use of contrasting color marks makes it easier to compare the relative values of our data.

We imagined a floor plan would immediately make the focus of this visualization clear: showing the resources a household has on average, when controlling for a key demographic factor. The above sketch shows a single household's resources, represented with furniture of various sizes and two colours. The size of each item of furniture is relative to the amount of financial assets that it represents, while the color it has denotes whether the asset is a source of income or an expenditure that household. The only shape with a certain meaning encoded is the bed - each one represents one person in a given household, and its shape is relative to how much that person earns annually. The idea is each furniture item is a solid color, only displaying a small label with the exact numerical value when moused over. Additionally, there is a legend that can also be moused over and links each furniture item to the table of values by highlighting. (Note: this particular household in the sketch does not receive any subsidies from the government, so only their total income is represented with the two beds).

Our preliminary brainstorming sketches.

To prepare for peer review, we created a slideshow to present mock interactions with our data. We assigned subsidies the color orange to denote a third source of income for households. In our final visualization, we made furniture grey when it does not receive that resource at all, and we create a different channel of sized marks to denote quantities of finances that a piece of furniture would represent. Here we decided to create multiple filtered views across our six dimensions and intended to implement brushing and linking between the furniture, the hexagon values, and the calculations pop-ups.

The full slideshow can be viewed here.

We received feedback that it was difficult for to discern what our central questions or investigation were meant to highlight from the data. We boil down to simpler marks and fewer channels for our final iteration. We hoped to show how households struggle, exploring how socioeconomic factors like educational attainment, marriage status, homeownership status, poverty level, and state can affect people's livelihoods and perspectives on wealth inequality across the country.

Conclusion

In conclusion, our project not only simplifies the complex narrative of housing inequality in the United States but also brings it to life through an interactive floor plan. By aggregating and processing extensive ACS SPM data, focusing on key demographic and economic aspects, and converting these into tangible, color-coded elements representing household conditions, we have created a tool that not only educates but also engages users in understanding and exploring the nuances of this critical social issue. This approach transforms abstract statistics into a relatable, visual story, making the realities of housing inequality more accessible and comprehensible to a broader audience. Our work underscores the power of data visualization in bridging the gap between complex data and public understanding, paving the way for more informed discussions and potential solutions to this persistent challenge.
